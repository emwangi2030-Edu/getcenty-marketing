Summary: This buyer guide gives Kenyan SME and mid-market teams a structured way to choose a payroll and payout stack in 2026 — a weighted scoring template across nine criteria (statutory compute, M-Pesa-native disbursement, casual-pay handling, accounting integration, cost transparency, support SLAs, audit and access, multi-country, exit), three worked SME profiles to anchor the scoring, an RFP question list to extract honest answers from vendors, and a verification rule that no claim enters the matrix without a written source. It is decision-support, not a vendor ranking, and not legal or tax advice; statutory specifics in Kenya change with finance law and KRA guidance, so confirm with a qualified advisor before applying any of it to a specific procurement.

Most "how to choose payroll software in Kenya" articles fail in one of two ways. The honest ones name vendors but freeze feature claims at the date of writing, leaving readers reading stale information six months later. The marketing-led ones tilt the comparison toward whoever paid for the post. This guide takes a different approach: it gives you the methodology and the evaluation template so you can run a fair evaluation yourself, at the moment you are buying, with the vendors you actually shortlist. The static facts that go stale stay out; the framework that survives stays in.

How to use this guide

This article is built around four artefacts you will produce as you work through an evaluation:

  1. A weighted criteria scorecard tailored to your workforce shape and statutory exposure.
  2. An RFP question list you send to each shortlisted vendor.
  3. A demo trace capturing what each vendor showed (and did not show) against your scenarios.
  4. A verification register recording the source for every claim that enters your scorecard — vendor email, public documentation page, signed RFP response, or demo evidence.

Before scoring, decide who is on your evaluation panel and how a final decision will be made. A panel of one is fast but biased; a panel of five is fair but slow. Most SMEs run with three: a finance lead (commercial owner), an operations lead (workflow owner), and a senior decision-maker (commercial sign-off). Document the panel and the decision rule before the first demo.

The methodology: weighted scoring across nine criteria

The criteria are drawn from payroll software Kenya: how to choose (the criteria pillar — read it first if you have not). The buyer guide turns those criteria into a scorecard. Each criterion gets a weight from 0 to 5 reflecting how much it matters for your business, and each vendor scores 0 to 5 against it. The weighted total ranks candidates without pretending the ranking is objective — your weights are your judgement, made explicit.

The nine criteria

  1. Statutory compute and rule maintenance. Does the vendor maintain Kenyan statutory rules under a documented release cadence, and how is back-dated correction handled?
  2. M-Pesa-native disbursement. Does the platform initiate, status-track, and reconcile M-Pesa payouts natively, or via export-import?
  3. Casual / hourly / piece-rate handling. Are non-monthly cycles a first-class concept with worker registers, attendance ingestion, and rate cards?
  4. Accounting integration. Is there a direct, maintained integration with your accounting platform that respects your chart of accounts?
  5. Cost transparency (3-year TCO). Are setup, escalations, modules, support tiers, and exit costs explicit in writing?
  6. Support SLAs (especially month-end). Are response and resolution times documented per severity, with month-end coverage commitments?
  7. Audit trail, access control, data governance. Field-level audit log, role-based permissions, data residency, security posture, breach notification.
  8. Multi-country scalability (weight 0 if Kenya-only). Native support for UG / TZ / RW / EA-wide statutory regimes.
  9. Exit terms and data portability. Standard formats, documented schemas, no penalty for migration.

The weighting step

Before any demos, fill in your weights:

  • 5 — Mission-critical; this criterion alone could disqualify a vendor.
  • 4 — Very important; a weak vendor on this criterion needs strong compensating factors elsewhere.
  • 3 — Important; weighed in the round.
  • 2 — Useful; tie-breaker territory.
  • 1 — Nice to have.
  • 0 — Not relevant to this business.

If every criterion is "5", you have not weighted; you have listed wishes. Force a distribution that reflects what matters most.

The scoring step (per vendor)

For each criterion and each vendor, score 0 to 5:

  • 5 — Verified strong support, demonstrated under your scenarios.
  • 4 — Verified support, minor caveats.
  • 3 — Claimed support, partial verification.
  • 2 — Partial or workaround-based support.
  • 1 — Claimed but not credibly verified.
  • 0 — No support, or vendor declined to demonstrate.

The verification rule applies in every cell: no score above 3 without a documented source. The source goes into your verification register alongside the score.

Notice how the same nine criteria produce very different shortlists when weighted by workforce reality. Profile A will rule out vendors that lack M-Pesa-native disbursement or treat casuals as a bolt-on. Profile B will rule out vendors without a maintained connector to its accounting platform. The RFP question list (send to each shortlisted vendor)

The fastest way to distinguish marketing from substance is a written RFP that demands specifics. Below is a starter list keyed to the nine criteria; expand or trim to match your context.

On statutory compute

  • Which Kenyan statutory deductions are computed natively by your engine today? Provide a screenshot or sandbox link.
  • What is your release cadence for statutory rule changes? Where is the changelog published?
  • How are back-dated corrections handled when a rule is clarified after a period has closed?
  • Who carries liability if the engine computes a statutory line incorrectly? Provide contract language.

On M-Pesa disbursement

  • Is M-Pesa disbursement initiated from within your platform, or via export to a separate tool? Demonstrate end-to-end in a sandbox.
  • What is captured per line on a batch (success / failure / retry / fallback)? Provide a sample response payload or report.
  • How is maker–checker enforced at a role level on disbursement?
  • What fallback rails are supported if M-Pesa fails?

On casual / hourly handling

  • How are casual workers represented in your data model — same as salaried, or a distinct entity?
  • Can attendance be ingested from mobile capture, paper roster scan, biometric, and supervisor app? Show each.
  • Can multiple cycle types run in parallel for the same employer?

On accounting integration

  • Which accounting platforms have direct integrations today? Provide screenshots of the journal export.
  • When was the connector last updated? Show the changelog.
  • Can the integration respect a custom chart of accounts? Demonstrate mapping UI.

On cost transparency

  • Provide a 3-year illustrative quote at our headcount assumptions, with separate lines for setup, base, add-ons, support tiers, and annual escalation.
  • List all add-on modules and which features they include.
  • Document exit costs in your standard contract.

On support SLAs

  • Provide a written SLA matrix (response, resolution, severity definitions, business hours, month-end coverage).
  • Who answers a P1 incident at 8pm on the last working day of the month?
  • Provide two reference customers willing to discuss real incidents.

On audit, access, and governance

  • Where is customer data stored and processed?
  • How is access controlled at field level for sensitive data (compensation, statutory IDs)?
  • Provide your latest third-party security attestation or pen-test summary.
  • What is your breach notification commitment in writing?

On multi-country

  • List the countries supported natively today, and the rule maintenance team per country.
  • How are multi-country statutory differences reconciled in one operational view?

On exit and portability

  • What is the data export format and schema documentation?
  • Are there penalty clauses for early termination or data export?
  • Are there technical lock-ins that make a migration harder than the contract suggests?

Send the same list to every shortlisted vendor on the same day. Score the responses on the same rubric. Document every reply that says "we are working on that" as a 0 or 1, not a 3 — a roadmap promise is not a verified capability.

Vendor categories: what to verify per bucket

The criteria pillar describes vendor categories in detail. The buyer-guide framing per category, with the verification step that gets a real answer:

  • Global HRIS-led platforms. Verify Kenyan statutory rule maintenance, M-Pesa-native disbursement, and casual-pay handling end-to-end. The most common gap is in these three areas.
  • Pan-African payroll platforms. Verify per-country team accountability, Kenya-specific depth, and disbursement integration with M-Pesa specifically (not generic mobile money).
  • Kenya-built / Africa-built payroll platforms (Workpay, WinguBox, Payroll.ke, Aren, Centy, and others). Verify: M-Pesa-native vs export integration, casual-pay depth, accounting connector freshness, support SLAs, and the rule-maintenance changelog. Do not assume parity within this category; differentiation is real.
  • Card-led and expense-focused platforms (Boya and similar). These complement payroll for expense; they are not a payroll replacement. Verify the integration between expense tooling and your chosen payroll platform.
  • Build-in-house. Verify the engineering team's commitment to statutory rule maintenance as a permanent line item, not a one-off project.

The verification rule applies to every cell of the matrix: a score above 3 requires a documented source — a vendor email, a public documentation URL, a signed RFP answer, or a demo recording with timestamp. Without sources, the matrix becomes opinion, and opinion is not a buyer guide.

What good vendor behaviour looks like during evaluation

  • RFP responses are written, signed, and on letterhead.
  • Demos use your data (or a representative subset), not the vendor's sandbox brochure data.
  • Vendors answer "we do not support that" cleanly when true, instead of pivoting to a roadmap line.
  • References are real customers you can speak to without intermediation.
  • Contract language matches RFP claims; commitments that are in the demo show up in the agreement.
  • Pricing is articulated in writing with no "trust us" gaps.
  • Implementation plans include parallel-running and a documented cutover plan.

The reverse — refused written SLAs, evasive answers, references the vendor handpicks rigidly, "custom development" for basic functionality, or surprise costs after signing — is a strong signal to walk away regardless of headline pricing.

Putting it together: the four-week evaluation rhythm

  1. Week 1: Framing. Panel, weights, RFP draft, longlist of 5–8 vendors.
  2. Week 2: RFPs out, demos scheduled. Same RFP to all vendors, same day. Demo slots booked in the same week to keep memory fresh.
  3. Week 3: Demos + scoring. Two-hour demos per vendor with your data. Score immediately after each demo; do not batch-score at the end of the week.
  4. Week 4: Shortlist + reference calls + decision. Top 2–3 advance to reference calls; commercial close happens with the winner; runner-up retained as backup in case of pre-contract surprises.

Four weeks is fast; six is more realistic with a full-time SME team. Anything under three weeks usually means corners are being cut on verification.

What to take away

A serious payroll-and-payout-stack decision in Kenya is a four-week, panel-led evaluation against weighted criteria, anchored by a written RFP, scored only on verified evidence, and finalised by reference calls. The criteria are workforce-shape dependent; the methodology is universal. Skip any step and the cost shows up in re-implementation, statutory exposure, or operational firefighting within two quarters of go-live. The matrix is decision-support, not a decision — the panel makes the call.
