Summary: A six-week migration playbook for Kenyan SMEs retiring the petty cash tin in favour of ledgered alternatives — M-Pesa float accounts, prepaid corporate cards, virtual cards for online spend, and banked supplier accounts. Includes a decision tree, a rollout plan, and the change-management traps that derail well-intentioned migrations. Operations guidance, not legal or tax advice; confirm statutory specifics with a qualified Kenyan advisor.

Petty cash is the most reliable source of reconciliation pain in a growing Kenyan SME. It works at five staff and breaks at fifty — sometimes much earlier, depending on how many sites and supervisors handle a float. This spoke sits under the pillar on expense management Kenya SMEs and gives you a concrete migration to the alternatives that actually work locally.

The case for retirement

Petty cash hides risk: custody risk (theft, loss), reconciliation risk (receipts of uneven quality), audit risk (gaps in evidence), statutory risk (VAT input claims weakened by missing TIM-compliant receipts), and operational risk (the custodian is on leave on the wrong day). The alternatives — M-Pesa float, prepaid cards, virtual cards, banked supplier accounts — turn each disbursement into a logged event with a statement, a counterparty, and a reference.

The four alternatives and where each fits

1. Dedicated M-Pesa float accounts

Best for: field operations, multi-site businesses, supervisors who already operate on M-Pesa, casual-pay tops-ups, supplier sends to non-banked counterparties.

  • One M-Pesa account per location or department, funded for operational spend.
  • Named operators with documented limits per operator and per transaction.
  • Daily reporting of disbursements with reasons captured against each send.
  • Statement becomes the petty-cash ledger.

Watch: SIM custody, number-rotation discipline, separation between personal and business numbers.

2. Prepaid corporate cards

Best for: office consumables, urgent supplier payments where the supplier accepts cards, regional or international travel, fuel for fleet vehicles.

  • Banks and fintechs in Kenya issue business prepaid cards with per-cardholder and per-merchant-category limits.
  • Statement-level evidence without the cash custody risk.
  • Lost-card workflow is faster than reissuing custody of cash.

Watch: ETIMS / VAT compliance — the card receipt does not replace the supplier's compliant invoice for VAT input claim.

3. Virtual cards for online and subscription spend

Best for: SaaS subscriptions, online advertising, marketplace purchases, single-use vendor payments.

  • Single-use or merchant-locked virtual cards remove subscription creep.
  • Prevent stale card numbers floating across services after staff turnover.
  • Statement reconciliation is clean because each card maps to one merchant or one purpose.

4. Banked supplier accounts

Best for: recurring suppliers, larger purchases, anyone willing to issue an invoice and accept terms.

  • Supplier invoices on credit terms; payment by EFT or PesaLink.
  • Replaces cash-on-delivery for everything except true micro-spend.
  • VAT-claim-friendly because compliant invoices are the default.

Watch: not all suppliers will accept terms initially; offer prepayment by EFT as an interim step while the relationship matures.

The decision tree

For any expense category about to flow through petty cash, ask in order:

  1. Can this be a banked supplier on terms? If yes, route there; this is the cleanest path.
  2. If not, can the supplier accept a card? Use the prepaid corporate card with documented evidence.
  3. If not, can payment be made over M-Pesa from a controlled float account? Use the M-Pesa float with reason captured.
  4. If none of the above, this is a true cash residual — ringfence with custodian, hard cap, weekly count, immediate posting.

The goal is to push categories up this tree over the migration window, not to eliminate cash on day one.

The six-week migration playbook

Week 1 — Inventory and policy

  • List every active petty-cash float (by site, custodian, balance, monthly throughput).
  • Categorise spend types flowing through each float over the last 3 months.
  • Draft the new policy: limits, approvers, evidence required, reimbursement cadence.
  • Identify the alternative for each category (banked / card / M-Pesa float / residual cash).

Week 2 — Open the alternatives

  • Open M-Pesa float accounts for each site or department as decided.
  • Issue prepaid cards to designated cardholders with documented limits.
  • Set up virtual cards for online and subscription spend.
  • Update supplier records: shift recurring suppliers to terms where possible.

Weeks 3–4 — Parallel running

  • New spend flows through alternatives.
  • Old petty-cash floats remain operational for true residual cash only.
  • Daily reporting of disbursements on each new rail.
  • Operations team trained on the policy and the new tools.

Week 5 — Reduce float balances

  • Reduce petty-cash float balances week-on-week; release the surplus to the bank.
  • Reconcile the new alternatives against the accounting system.
  • Surface issues — refusals from suppliers, training gaps, policy edge cases — and resolve.

Week 6 — Close and retain

  • Close most petty-cash floats; keep only the documented residual where genuinely needed.
  • Final reconciliation of closed floats — no unexplained balance.
  • Archive the migration documentation for audit.
  • Schedule a 30-day retrospective and a 90-day review.

Change-management traps

  • Forcing the migration onto cash-only suppliers. Some suppliers (small kiosks, casual contractors) genuinely operate cash-only; insisting they accept M-Pesa or cards can damage operations. Ringfence true residual cash rather than fight it.
  • Underestimating training time. Site supervisors with five years of habit do not adopt new tools in a single demo. Schedule recurring touchpoints in weeks 3 and 5.
  • Custody overconfidence on cards. Prepaid cards have lower custody risk than cash but are not zero-risk; lost cards must be cancelled fast.
  • Ignoring VAT realities. Card receipts do not replace ETIMS-compliant invoices for VAT input claim. Update supplier education accordingly.
  • Cancelling old floats too fast. Operational continuity matters; allow parallel running long enough that no team gets stranded.
