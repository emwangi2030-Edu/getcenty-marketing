Three statutory components — NSSF, SHIF, and NITA — recur in every conversation about Kenyan payroll, alongside PAYE and the Housing levy. They are categorically different from each other in purpose, in who is in scope, and in how often they change. Why this article does not quote rates

Two reasons. First, rates and thresholds for these deductions in Kenya have changed multiple times in recent years through finance laws, regulations, and agency notices; a number published today may be stale in months. Second, the cost of a stale number in a guidance article is higher than the cost of pointing readers to authoritative sources — payroll teams that anchor on the wrong figure miscalculate quietly until an audit notices.

The discipline that matters is dated and reviewed updates, not blog-quoted figures. What you need to know about each deduction — what it is for, when it applies, where it sits in the cycle — is framing this article can provide responsibly.

NSSF — the National Social Security Fund

Purpose. NSSF is the statutory social security scheme intended to provide retirement and related benefits to Kenyan workers. Contributions are made by both the employer and the employee, calculated against the employee's pensionable earnings under the tier structure in force at the time.

Where it sits in payroll. NSSF is deducted from the employee's gross pay and remitted alongside the employer contribution under the statutory schedule. The deduction appears on the payslip as an employee contribution; the employer contribution is a separate expense line in the books.

Who is in scope. The scope of NSSF applies broadly to employees in formal engagements, with treatment of casual, part-time, and short-term engagements depending on the specifics of the engagement and the rules in force. Confirm with your statutory reviewer whether each category of casual or hourly engagement in your business is in scope at the rates currently in force.

What changes. NSSF contribution rates and tier structures have been the subject of legislative and regulatory revision. Stay current via the NSSF official communications, the Kenya Gazette, and your payroll vendor's published rule-update changelog.

SHIF — the Social Health Insurance Fund

Purpose. SHIF is the statutory health-insurance contribution scheme, intended to fund universal health coverage for Kenyan workers and dependants. Like NSSF, it is deducted from the employee's earnings and remitted to the fund under rules in force.

Where it sits in payroll. SHIF is computed against the relevant earnings base (confirm the current base with your statutory reviewer) and deducted from gross pay. The deduction appears on the payslip and is remitted alongside other statutory lines.

Who is in scope. SHIF generally applies to employees; treatment of casual, part-time, and short-term engagements depends on the rules in force and the specifics of the engagement. Statutory-reviewer documentation is essential — many SMEs misclassify casuals on the SHIF question and discover the gap during audit.

What changes. SHIF is relatively recent in the Kenyan statutory landscape, having succeeded prior National Hospital Insurance Fund arrangements. Expect ongoing regulatory clarification and align your payroll engine with the current rules through vendor releases and statutory-reviewer sign-off.

NITA — the National Industrial Training Authority levy

Purpose. NITA is an industrial training levy intended to fund employee training and development in Kenya. Unlike NSSF and SHIF, NITA is generally an employer-paid levy (rather than an employee deduction) calculated against the workforce.

Where it sits in payroll. NITA is calculated and remitted by the employer; it does not typically appear as a payslip deduction but does appear in the employer's statutory expense schedule.

Who is in scope. NITA applies to employers in scope under the relevant Act; specifics on who is captured, exemptions, and the calculation base depend on the rules in force. Confirm with your statutory reviewer.

What changes. NITA rules and reimbursement arrangements (training grants that the levy funds) evolve. Many SMEs underuse the reimbursement side; statutory reviewers can identify whether your training spend qualifies for partial recovery.

Where these sit alongside PAYE and the Housing levy

NSSF, SHIF, and NITA do not exist in isolation. A complete Kenyan payroll statutory schedule typically includes:

  • PAYE — Pay As You Earn income tax, deducted from employee earnings and remitted to KRA under the current bands and relief schedule.
  • Housing levy — a more recent addition, with both employer and employee components.
  • NSSF — covered above.
  • SHIF — covered above.
  • NITA — covered above (employer-paid).
  • Sector-specific levies — applicable to certain industries (for example, regulatory or training-board levies in specific sectors).

What "good" looks like in payroll software for statutory deductions

When evaluating payroll software against the statutory question, ask:

  • Is the rule maintenance vendor-managed? When the rate or tier changes, does the vendor publish a release that updates the engine, or are you expected to reconfigure manually?
  • Where is the changelog? A vendor that updates the engine without a published changelog leaves auditors without an explanation when a rate suddenly changes mid-year.
  • Can you see the calculation per employee, per period? An audit query two years later should be answerable from the system, not from a recomputed spreadsheet.
  • How are back-dated changes handled? Agencies sometimes clarify rules months after the fact; the engine must support corrections without rewriting the historical record.
  • Are casual / hourly / piece-rate scopes configurable? The scope decision is a statutory-reviewer call; the engine must enforce it without exception.
  • Does the vendor accept liability if the engine miscalculates a statutory line? Contracts vary; this is a question for procurement and legal, not for the demo.

The refresh discipline you should run on this topic

Whatever you publish or rely on internally about NSSF / SHIF / NITA rates and thresholds should carry:

  • A visible last reviewed date.
  • A named statutory reviewer who owns the periodic refresh.
  • A calendar reminder to refresh quarterly and on any finance-law or agency change.
  • A pointer to the authoritative source (the agency's published material) rather than secondary commentary.
