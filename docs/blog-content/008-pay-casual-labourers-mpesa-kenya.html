Paying casual labourers via M-Pesa works on the same disbursement rails as salaried staff, but the cadence, batch size, and failure profile are different enough that copy-pasting a monthly-payroll process does not work. This spoke sits under the pillar on casual workers payments Kenya and walks through one cycle end-to-end, naming the records and checks that make the difference between a clean Friday and a Saturday morning of phone calls.

The cycle, in one sentence

Attendance is captured at the worksite, locked at a published cutoff, computed against rate cards, reviewed maker–checker, sent as a batch on M-Pesa with line-level status, failures triaged inside the same day, and reconciled before the next cycle opens.

Step 1 — Attendance capture at source

The single biggest determinant of casual-pay accuracy is capture at the source, not at the end of the period:

  • Mobile app for supervisors: tap-to-mark-present, photo log of who is on site, geo-tagged where the workforce is field-based.
  • Paper roster as a fallback: countersigned twice a day, photographed by the supervisor at lunch and end of shift.
  • Biometric or QR check-in where the site supports it: removes ambiguity for shifts with set start and end times.
  • Supervisor sign-off at end of period is mandatory, regardless of which capture method is in use.

Avoid: WhatsApp text messages as the only attendance record. They are easy to lose, easy to dispute, and impossible to audit at scale.

Step 2 — Rate computation

For each worker on the attendance list, compute the gross from attendance × rate card or output × piece rate:

  • Rate cards are first-class objects: a documented rate with an effective date per role, archived rather than overwritten when changed.
  • Allowances (transport, food, hazard) added per worker per period under documented policy.
  • Deductions (tools, breakages, advances) applied under the deductions register, with a per-worker reconciliation.
  • Statutory deductions applied where the casual category is in scope per your statutory reviewer's documented decision.

Step 3 — MSISDN hygiene

Validate every M-Pesa number before the batch leaves preparation:

  • Strip whitespace, confirm country code, validate digit count.
  • Flag duplicates within the batch (one worker, two lines — investigate before sending).
  • Flag numbers that failed in the previous cycle.
  • Reject changes made within the cutoff window without a second approver — number-changing scams target casual workforces specifically.

A weekly casual batch with poor MSISDN hygiene typically sees 5–15% failure rate; a disciplined process gets it under 2%.

Step 4 — Maker–checker review

One person prepares the batch (worker list, amounts, totals); a different person approves it. In a small team:

  • The preparer signs off in writing or in-system.
  • The approver spot-checks at least one exception case in detail.
  • Approval is recorded with date-time and approver identity.
  • Where roles must blend on a particular cycle, the deviation is documented and reviewed quarterly.

Step 5 — Submission and per-line response

Submit the batch on the contracted M-Pesa rail. Capture the provider's reference and the per-line response — success, failure, pending. See M-Pesa B2C payroll disbursement Kenya for the technical detail on idempotency, partial success, and retry semantics.

Step 6 — Same-day failure triage

For each failure:

  1. Identify the worker and confirm the correct number with the supervisor.
  2. Attempt one retry on the same line reference if the failure reason supports it.
  3. If retry fails, decide: pay through an alternate rail today, or defer to the next cycle with an approved exception note.
  4. Communicate with the worker — uncertainty drives the next-day call volume.

Do not let failures float overnight without a decision. Open failures past 24 hours are the strongest predictor of disputes.

Step 7 — Proof and posting

  • Per-worker proof of payment recorded against the batch ID — provider reference, amount, timestamp.
  • Site / supervisor receives the batch summary for posting on the noticeboard or sharing in the team channel.
  • Journal entry posted to the ledger same cycle, carrying the batch ID in the narration.

Common operator mistakes

  • Editing attendance after cutoff without a documented exception — corrodes the audit trail.
  • Skipping rate-card archive when rates change — makes historical disputes impossible to resolve.
  • Treating MSISDN failures as the worker's problem rather than triaging same-cycle — destroys trust quickly.
  • Running advances through WhatsApp instead of the deductions register — they get forgotten.
  • Conflating disbursement with statutory — paying the worker today does not file the statutory line; finance must own both.
